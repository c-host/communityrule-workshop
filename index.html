Using Digital Tools to Create Community Rules Together

A Hands-on Governance Workshop

Workshop Overview

A 2-hour hands-on workshop exploring how groups can use digital tools to create clear rules for self-governing their communities. Governance is how groups make choices and organize themselves - like a recipe that helps everyone work together effectively.

By the end of the workshop, participants will have learned how to create and publish an example community governance structure with CommunityRule that they can use and adapt for their community projects.

    What is Self-Governance?

    Self-governance is how groups make choices and organize themselves. It's about creating clear ways of working together that everyone can understand and participate in.

    Why Rules and Self-Governance Matter

    Introducing Jo Freeman

    Background on Jo Freeman: Feminist activist, political scientist, and key figure in the women's liberation movement in the United States of America. Wrote this influential essay in 1970 after observing that "structureless" groups often created hidden hierarchies.

    Making Hidden Hierarchies Visible

    For everyone to have the opportunity to be involved in a given group and to participate in its activities the structure must be explicit, not implicit. The rules of decision-making must be open and available to everyone, and this can happen only if they are formalized.
    — Jo Freeman, "The Tyranny of Structurelessness"

    The Problem with "Structureless" Groups

    Groups that claim to be "structureless" often create hidden hierarchies through informal friendship networks, making power invisible and excluding some members from decision-making.

    There is no such thing as a structureless group. Any group of people of whatever nature that comes together for any length of time for any purpose will inevitably structure itself in some fashion.
    — Jo Freeman, "The Tyranny of Structurelessness"

    The Approach: Democratic Structuring

    Democratic structuring with explicit rules, roles, and decision-making processes ensures that everyone can participate meaningfully.

    Jo Freeman's Seven Principles of Democratic Structuring

    Once the movement no longer clings tenaciously to the ideology of 'structurelessness,' it is free to develop those forms of organization best suited to its healthy functioning. This does not mean that we should go to the other extreme and blindly imitate the traditional forms of organization. But neither should we blindly reject them all. Some of the traditional techniques will prove useful, albeit not perfect; some will give us insights into what we should and should not do to obtain certain ends with minimal costs to the individuals in the movement. Mostly, we will have to experiment with different kinds of structuring and develop a variety of techniques to use for different situations. [...] before we can proceed to experiment intelligently, we must accept the idea that there is nothing inherently bad about structure itself -- only its excess use.
    — Jo Freeman, "The Tyranny of Structurelessness"

    1. Delegate Authority

    Delegate specific authority through democratic procedures

    2. Ensure Accountability

    Make those with authority accountable to the group

    3. Distribute Power

    Distribute authority widely to prevent power monopolies

    4. Rotate Tasks

    Rotate tasks among members (but not too frequently)

    5. Match Abilities

    Allocate tasks based on ability, interest and responsibility

    6. Share Information

    Share information widely and frequently

    7. Equal Access

    Ensure equal access to group resources and skills

    Note:

    Even organizations that explicitly aim to be non-hierarchical, like Teal organizations, Holacracy, or those using flat ontology, still have clear structures and processes.

    For Example

  • Teal organizations use self-managing teams with defined roles and decision-making protocols.
  • Holacracy replaces traditional hierarchies with "circles" that have explicit governance rules.
  • Flat ontologies still require coordination mechanisms and ways to distribute responsibility.

    • These approaches don't eliminate structure - they reimagine it to be more democratic and participatory while maintaining clarity and accountability.

    Introducing CommunityRule: Examples

    What is CommunityRule?

    "CommunityRule is a governance toolkit for great communities."

    Purpose

    The purpose of CommunityRule is to help communities establish appropriate norms for decision-making, stewardship, and culture.

    Who Made It

    CommunityRule is a project of the Media Enterprise Design Lab at the University of Colorado Boulder, in collaboration with Metagov. It is primarily authored by MEDLab director Nathan Schneider and Drew Hornbein.

    Example CommunityRule Projects

    Kids Cooperation School Classroom (DWeb Camp 2022)

    Summary: "There are kids and adults and teachers. there's a kid principal. it looks like a normal school. There are about 500 students. The campus is big enough to walk around."

    Key Point: This is a tool that children can use to imagine and represent their ideas about how organizations should be run. Your rules don't have to be perfect and can also be about imagining what you or your community think would be best.

    Video: Kids Creating Their Own Governance

    Video Point: Watch how children use CommunityRule to design their own school governance structure at DWeb Camp 2022.

    You and Me

    Summary: "This is how we relate to each other."

    Key Point: Poetic and concise. Not every Rule needs to be complicated. Great example of making something implicit explicit.

    Hard Fork

    Summary: CommunityRule used as a summary and friendlier version of the organization's bylaws (legally binding organization laws).

    Key Point: Highly detailed, but this is not necessary. Done by a mature organization. Good example of structure redundancy and accessibility with bi-directional linking with Bylaws.

    An Artist Hang Out

    Summary: A loosely defined but clear structure that allows flexibility in how rules are enforced, while also making clear how the organization operates.

    Key Point: Something like this level of detail might be a good starting point for today's exercises. Shows how to balance structure with flexibility.

    Exercise 1 - Mapping Current Groups | 0:20 - 0:45

    Overview

  • Individual activity: Think about a group you're part of
  • Small group talk: Compare your ideas

    • Individual Activity [10 Minutes]

    Exercise:

    Use the worksheet to write down your ideas about how a group you are in is currently organized

    Prompts:

  • What are the ways your group works together?
  • What rules or habits are important to the group?
  • What's important to you?

    • Example

    A group of friends who meet to talk about books. Here are their ways of working together:
  • Everyone brings a book
  • Everyone reads the book before meeting
  • Everyone talks about the book
  • Everyone helps choose the next book
  • Everyone comes to the meeting

    • Focus Areas:

  • Culture (Group Culture) - What's important to the group?
  • Decision (Making Decisions) - How does the group choose things?
  • Process (Getting Things Done) - How does the group do what it decides?
  • Structure (Group Structure) - Who does what?

    • Small Group Discussion [15 Minutes]

    Exercise:

    Come together in groups of 3-4 and discuss what you wrote.

    Prompt:

    What's similar and different in how your groups work together?

    Sharings

  • One person from each small group shares group reflections on insights and patterns with everyone
    • Exercise 2: Designing and Implementing New Group Rules | 0:45 - 1:40

    Overview

  • Step-by-step review of CommunityRule
  • Small group activity: Design a group that would work well

    • Step-by-Step Review of CommunityRule [5 Minutes]

    Let's explore how to use CommunityRule together:

    Key Steps:

    1. Visit the homepage and explore the interface
    2. Click "Create" to start a new Rule
    3. Add the four main areas: Culture, Decision, Process, Structure
    4. Add details to each area with clear descriptions
    5. Use clear, simple language that everyone can understand
    6. Write in whichever language you prefer

    Small Group Activity: Design A New Group [15 Minutes]

    Exercise:

    Gather in groups of 3-4 and design a new or modify an already existing group that would work well for everyone.

    Prompt:

    What governance (ways of working together) would be important?

    Design Steps:

    Use the workshop handout to:

    1. Name and describe your group
    2. Add the four main areas: Culture, Decision, Process, Structure
    3. Add details to each area with clear descriptions
    4. Write it down using clear, simple language

    Focus Areas:

  • Culture (Group Culture) - What's important to the group?
  • Decision (Making Decisions) - How does the group choose things?
  • Process (Getting Things Done) - How does the group do what it decides?
  • Structure (Group Structure) - Who does what?

    • Writing Tips

  • Write what's realistic, not just what's ideal
  • Use consistent terminology throughout
  • Plan for evolution and changes
  • Make it easy to understand for everyone
  • Start with a simple example from real life or create your own
  • Share and combine ideas

    • Turn paper ideas into CommunityRule [25 minutes]

    Option 1:

    Exercise:

    With your group, turn your paper ideas into a Rule using CommunityRule.

    Option 2:

    Exercise

    Work with one small group to show everyone how they can turn their paper ideas into a Rule using CommunityRule

    Optional Step:

    Publish New Rules to CommunityRule

    Note: participants are able to publish their creations publicly to the Library, but they are not obligated to. Library is still an experimental feature and deleting a Rule requires contacting the administrators.

    Large Group Discussion [10 Minutes]

    Sharings

    Small groups discuss:

  • Their CommunityRule
  • Discuss major choices
  • Share what worked well and what was challenging
  • Identify emerging patterns and common approaches
    • Open Discussion and Wrap-up | 1:40 - 2:00

    A closing discussion to share governance best practices, identify next steps for participants, and gather workshop feedback through Q&A.
